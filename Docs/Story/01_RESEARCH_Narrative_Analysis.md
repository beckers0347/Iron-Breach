# WHAT MAKES GAME STORIES LAST
## A Research Analysis of Narrative Design in Landmark Video Games
*Iron Breach pre-production document · Part 1 of 2 · July 2026*
*Companion document: `02_IRONBREACH_Narrative_Bible.md` (applies every law in this file)*

---

## 0. Method and Scope

This document studies the games whose stories players still argue about years or decades after release, and extracts the repeatable techniques behind them. Each section ends with **extracted laws** — numbered rules that the Iron Breach narrative bible directly applies. Case studies draw from: FromSoftware's catalog (Demon's Souls through Elden Ring), Destiny 1/2's ten-year saga, Halo, Half-Life, BioShock, Mass Effect, Metal Gear Solid, Silent Hill 2, The Last of Us, Red Dead Redemption 2, God of War (2018), Hollow Knight, Outer Wilds, NieR: Automata, Warframe, Final Fantasy XIV, World of Warcraft, Titanfall 2, Control, Portal, Spec Ops: The Line, Subnautica, Disco Elysium, KOTOR, Fez/Tunic, and Clair Obscur: Expedition 33 — plus the documented failures of Anthem, Lightfall-era Destiny, Halo 5, and others.

A note on what "story" means in games. Academic and GDC literature converges on the same point: game narrative is not a script delivered to a viewer, it is **evidence arranged for an investigator**. Environmental storytelling scholarship describes it as arranging objects so they *suggest* a story the player reconstructs — "more collaborative than performative." That single idea — the player as archaeologist rather than audience — underlies almost everything below.

## 1. How Great Games Introduce Their Worlds

**Half-Life 2 — the boot, not the war.** Valve never explains the Seven Hour War or the Combine occupation. You step off a train into City 17 and learn everything from texture: a floating camera drone photographs your face, a guard knocks a can to the floor and makes you pick it up. Oppression is taught through a *verb*, not a cutscene. The lesson: introduce a world by making the player *perform* their place in it.

**BioShock — one perfect descent.** Rapture is introduced in a bathysphere: a single continuous shot through a whale-passing cityscape while Andrew Ryan's recorded speech plays. Three tools stacked — motion (descent), voice (ideology), and vista (scale) — deliver a decade of backstory in ninety seconds without a single "as you know" line. The descent itself became the franchise's signature verb (BioShock Infinite inverts it with an ascension).

**Dark Souls / Elden Ring — myth first, facts never.** FromSoftware openings are deliberately useless as exposition: a cinematic narrates names you cannot retain (Gwyn, the Witch of Izalith, the Furtive Pygmy) over images you cannot yet parse. This is intentional. The opening plants *mythic vocabulary* that the player will spend a hundred hours slowly attaching meaning to. The world is introduced as a question you don't yet know how to ask. Critically, the *geography does the exposition*: Elden Ring's Erdtree is visible from nearly everywhere — the game's thesis statement rendered as skybox.

**The Last of Us — lose something first.** The prologue gives you Sarah for twenty minutes and takes her away. Every subsequent worldbuilding detail (checkpoints, ration cards, Fireflies graffiti) lands harder because the world has already *cost you something*. Worlds are not introduced through information; they are introduced through loss.

**Halo: Combat Evolved — the world is the mystery.** The ring is the title, the setting, and the question. "What is Halo?" powers seven missions before any answer arrives, and the answer (it's a weapon, not a refuge) reverses the emotional meaning of the ground you've been walking on.

**Outer Wilds — curiosity as tutorial.** No quest markers; knowledge is literally the progression system. The world introduces itself at exactly the speed of the player's own curiosity — the most elegant introduction model in the medium, but it only works when every mystery has a genuinely satisfying answer waiting.

**Destiny — the cautionary hybrid.** "You've been dead a long time" is a superb cold open, but vanilla Destiny (2014) then hid its actual worldbuilding in out-of-game Grimoire cards. The community famously mocked "I don't have time to explain why I don't have time to explain." Lesson: in-medias-res only works if the medium itself carries the payoff. (Bungie course-corrected with in-game lore tabs from Forsaken onward — and player trust measurably followed.)

### Extracted Laws
- **L1. Introduce worlds through verbs, not paragraphs.** The player's first actions should *be* the exposition (pick up the can, descend in the sphere, carry the wounded).
- **L2. Front-load myth, back-load facts.** An opening may name things it refuses to explain — provided the game spends years honoring that debt.
- **L3. Charge the world emotionally before explaining it intellectually.** Loss first, lore second.
- **L4. Make the central mystery visible from everywhere** — a skybox-scale question (the ring, the tree, the sealed city) the player never stops seeing.
- **L5. Never outsource core mystery to out-of-game material.** External lore may deepen, never carry.

## 2. How Mystery Is Built Over Time

**FromSoftware — the shattered book.** Dark Souls distributes its story as fragments: item descriptions, armor flavor, NPC half-sentences, architecture. No single fragment is a story; the *assembly* is. Scholarship on Dark Souls notes that surface players still receive a coherent surface story, while "only through careful analysis and connecting the dots can the player uncover the real story" — a two-track design that never punishes the casual player for the deep player's feast. The deliberate gaps are load-bearing: ambiguity is not missing content, it is the engine that runs the community for a decade.

**Destiny — the lore economy.** Destiny's decade ran on a simple loop: every answer ships with two new questions. The Books of Sorrow (Taken King, 2015) answered "what are the Hive?" and planted "what is the Deep?" — which took *nine years* to pay off. Raid-gated lore (only obtainable through the hardest content) made knowledge itself prestigious. Unveiling (2019) introduced an unreliable cosmic narrator whose every claim the community still litigates. The franchise's late-era mistake is equally instructive: The Final Shape (2024) closed the saga well but demonstrated that **full closure kills theorizing** — Steam player counts collapsed ~90% within weeks partly because, with the mystery answered, the conversation ended.

**Warframe — the vaulted secret.** For three years Warframe never explained what the Tenno actually were. The Second Dream (2015) answered it in a way that recontextualized every mission the player had ever run. The technique: **hold one enormous secret in absolute silence while shipping constant small mysteries around it.** Players forgave years of quiet because the eventual answer proved the silence was *authored*, not empty.

**Control — the redaction as promise.** Remedy's found-document design (SCP-inspired case files, ██████ redactions, Threshold Kids puppet videos) demonstrates that *withholding style* matters: a redaction bar is a visible, deliberate promise that an answer exists and is being kept from you by someone in-fiction. Mystery gains menace when concealment has an author inside the world.

**FFXIV — the seed vault.** Final Fantasy XIV plants payoffs entire expansions early: the hymn "Answers" (2013) contains lyrics whose true speaker is only revealed in Shadowbringers/Endwalker (2019–2021). Eight-year-old song lyrics became evidence. This is the **long-seed doctrine**: put the truth in plain sight, in a form nobody can parse yet, and the reveal converts your entire back catalog into foreshadowing.

**Clair Obscur: Expedition 33 — the modern control case.** 2025's narrative darling shows both edges: its central twist is widely praised for capturing grief precisely *because* the game commits everything to it — but critics documented the cost of "mysteries within mysteries" that stack without interim payoffs. Mystery needs a payment schedule, not just a balloon payment.

### Extracted Laws
- **L6. Two-track everything:** a surface story a tired player can follow, and a deep story that rewards archaeology. Neither track may insult the other.
- **L7. Every answer ships with new questions** — but on a *published rhythm*: small payoffs constantly, medium payoffs per season/expansion, one giant payoff per era.
- **L8. Keep one Vault Secret** — a single enormous truth held in total silence for years. Never leak it, never tease it cheaply, and make its reveal recontextualize everything before it.
- **L9. Give concealment an author.** Redactions, classified stamps, missing pages — someone *in the world* is hiding this, and that someone is a character.
- **L10. Total closure is death.** End arcs, never end the mystery. Always leave a sacred unanswered core.

## 3. How Plot Twists Are Foreshadowed

**BioShock — "Would you kindly."** The most cited twist in games works because the foreshadowing is *diegetic and functional*: the trigger phrase is spoken openly across the whole game, hidden inside politeness — and the twist indicts the *player*: obeying objective markers was the mind control. The medium's unique twist space is **complicity** — recontextualizing not what the player watched, but what the player *did*.

**KOTOR — Revan.** Foreshadowed through mechanical texture (your inexplicable combat aptitude), NPC observations, and dreams. The classic "amnesia twist" done honestly: the clues were available, the reveal is checkable.

**Spec Ops: The Line — the slow indictment.** Loading-screen text drifts from tips to accusations ("Do you feel like a hero yet?"). The twist is less an event than a gradient — the game was never lying, the player was choosing not to read.

**Silent Hill 2 — the degrading letter.** Mary's letter, the object that motivates the entire journey, physically fades as James's denial erodes; by endgame the envelope is blank. Foreshadowing carried by an *inventory item's texture*. Nobody notices on a first run; everyone gasps on the second.

**Outer Wilds — total-availability foreshadowing.** Everything is discoverable in any order; the "twist" is only the moment your model of the system snaps into place. The purest form of the rule: **a twist is an answer to a question the player didn't know they were asking.**

**FFX, NieR: Automata, Braid** — structural foreshadowing: the *form* of the game (a narrated memory, repeated routes, reversed time) is itself the clue.

**The test of a fair twist** used across all of these: on replay, the twist must look *inevitable* — and the best game twists make the player's own hands part of the evidence.

### Extracted Laws
- **L11. Foreshadow in at least three channels** — dialogue, environment/props, and *mechanics* — so the reveal is checkable in replay ("the clues were always there").
- **L12. The strongest game twists recontextualize the player's own actions**, not just the plot. Complicity beats surprise.
- **L13. Hide clues in function, not in secrecy** — the trigger phrase spoken politely, the letter you carry, the tapping finger in every cutscene. In plain sight, wearing camouflage of normalcy.
- **L14. Never twist away something the player emotionally owns** (see §10 — the Halo 5 rule). Twists spend trust; foreshadowing is how you pre-fund the account.

## 4. How Characters Become Iconic

Analysis across GLaDOS, Cortana, Handsome Jack, Arthas, The Boss (MGS3), Ellie & Joel, Kratos (2018), BT-7274, Solaire, Sans, and Destiny's Cayde-6/Eris Morn yields a consistent five-part pattern:

1. **Silhouette + voice.** Iconic characters are recognizable in shadow and quotable in one line ("The cake is a lie," "Hey. Trust me."). Distinct speech rhythm matters more than dialogue volume.
2. **A load-bearing contradiction.** GLaDOS: comedian *and* murderer. The Boss: the enemy *and* the moral center. Kratos: the god-killer *afraid of his own son learning who he is*. Cayde: the joker whose ledger is full of debts to the dead. One clean contradiction generates years of fan analysis.
3. **Mechanic fusion.** The character's personality lives in a gameplay verb. BT-7274 catches you mid-air; the *trust* is played, not stated — which is why "Protocol 3: Protect the Pilot" devastates. GLaDOS *is* the test chambers. Cortana is quite literally in your head, the mission voice — which is why her later corruption lands as a personal violation. **If a character has no gameplay verb, they will not become iconic.**
4. **Scarcity.** Iconic characters are under-exposed. The Boss appears in perhaps forty minutes of MGS3. Solaire has maybe a dozen lines. Overexposure is how live-service games grind legends into furniture.
5. **Vulnerability at scale.** Gods with wounds: the immortal with dementia, the AI who was a person, the legend who cannot save his own partner. Power draws attention; the crack in it draws love.

**The companion corollary (Ellie, Elizabeth, Atreus, BT):** long-form bonds form through *shared mundane time* — boat rides, elevator talks, camp chores — not through set pieces. God of War (2018) and RDR2 proved downtime is where attachment actually accrues.

### Extracted Laws
- **L15. Every major character needs: one silhouette, one voice-rhythm, one contradiction, one gameplay verb, one wound.** Write these five before writing any dialogue.
- **L16. Ration your legends.** Screen-time scarcity is a preservative.
- **L17. Attachment accrues in downtime.** Schedule quiet, mechanically-light shared time with any character the story will later spend.

## 5. How Villains Become Memorable

**Handsome Jack (Borderlands 2)** — proximity and pettiness. He calls you *personally*, mid-mission, for the whole game; he is funny, wounded, and utterly convinced he's the hero. **Andrew Ryan (BioShock)** — an ideology so coherent it built a city, delivering the medium's defining scene by *choosing* the manner of his own death to prove a thesis ("A man chooses; a slave obeys"). **The Lich King/Arthas (Warcraft)** — a fall the player witnessed across games; WotLK then let him *personally haunt the leveling experience*, appearing in zone stories long before the raid. **Sovereign (Mass Effect)** — one conversation on Virmire reframes the entire series: not hatred, *indifference* at cosmic scale ("We are each a nation."). **SHODAN, Kefka, Sephiroth** — the violation principle: each takes something irreplaceable from the player (autonomy; the world itself; Aerith — the party member whose menu slot stays empty). **Micah (RDR2)** — no cosmic scale at all; memorable purely because he costs Arthur everything and is *small* about it. **Vaas (Far Cry 3)** — a performance so specific it outlived its game.

The pattern:
1. **They are right about something.** The best villains carry a truth the heroes haven't answered — Ryan on choice, Sovereign on scale, Jack on the bandits actually being dangerous. Players argue *for* great villains online.
2. **They act between missions.** Asymmetric agency — they speak when they choose, strike where you aren't. The world visibly changes because of them.
3. **They cost the player something personal and irreversible.** Not "the world" — a *menu slot*, a companion, a home base. (Destiny understood this when Ghaul killed the player's home and powers in the first mission of D2; Forsaken understood it better when it killed Cayde.)
4. **Intimacy.** The villain knows *you*, addresses *you*, and ideally has been *with you* the whole time (GLaDOS, Jack in your ear, the letter in your pocket).
5. **A non-human villain needs a voice or a face by proxy** — cosmology alone doesn't threaten; the Reapers needed Sovereign and Saren; the Darkness eventually needed the Witness. A faceless force must be given ambassadors.

### Extracted Laws
- **L18. Give the villain a defensible thesis.** If half the forum can argue the villain was right, you've built a decade of discourse.
- **L19. The villain must take something the UI itself remembers** — an empty roster slot, a renamed hub, a scar on the player's own equipment.
- **L20. Cosmic threats need personal ambassadors.** Scale dreads; faces menace. Use both, and let the face speak to the player by name.

## 6. Where Lore Hides — the Distribution System

The masterworks treat lore placement as a *loot economy*. Documented systems, by channel:

| Channel | Landmark example | Design principle |
|---|---|---|
| **Item descriptions** | Dark Souls/Elden Ring | Every item is one sentence of a burned book; rarity gates depth |
| **Audio logs** | System Shock → BioShock → Prey/Control | A dead person's voice in a specific place; the room contradicts or completes the recording |
| **Environment tableaux** | Fallout skeleton dioramas; Hollow Knight's corpse-choked halls | A frozen scene implying a verb; no text at all |
| **Architecture itself** | Elden Ring's divine topography; Halo's Forerunner scale | The building *is* the argument — who builds like this, and why? |
| **Mission incidentals** | Half-Life 2 checkpoint theater; Titanfall 2 factory tour | Worldbuilding staged in the periphery of an objective |
| **Raid/endgame gating** | Destiny raids (Vault of Glass, Last Wish) | Hardest content carries deepest truth; *mechanics are the lore* — VoG's time-splitting encounters teach Vex nature better than any codex |
| **Flavor text on gear** | Destiny weapon quips; Warframe leverian | One sentence of someone's life on an object you use ten thousand times |
| **Classified documents** | Control's case files; MGS codec | Redaction as authored concealment (see L9) |
| **NPC ambient dialogue** | RDR2 camp chatter; FFXIV patch NPCs | The world reacts to the story on a delay, like real gossip |
| **Optional dungeons/side content** | Bloodborne chalices; Witcher 3 contracts | Side content holds *sharper* truth than the main line — reward curiosity with candor |
| **Out-of-game paratext** | Destiny ARGs, Fez/Tunic ciphers, Halo terminals | Community-scale puzzles; see §7 — deepen, never carry (L5) |

Two structural rules recur. First, a **density gradient**: roughly, the majority of narrative should be receivable passively, a large minority findable with effort, and a deliberate remainder *never confirmed at all* — the permanent argument fuel. Second, **placement is authorship**: an audio log's meaning is its *location* (the confession found in the nursery, not the office). GDC's environmental-storytelling literature calls this "leaving traces" — indices arranged so players infer cause, and infer *character* from what someone chose to hide where.

### Extracted Laws
- **L21. Build a lore rarity ladder** mirroring the loot ladder: common ambient → uncommon logs → rare files → raid-exclusive truths → never-confirmed apocrypha.
- **L22. Location is half the text.** Write every collectible as *object + placement + contradiction between them*.
- **L23. Mechanics are the deepest lore channel.** The boss's rules should teach the cosmology (what it protects, what it fears, how it thinks) without a single word.
- **L24. Side content gets the sharpest truths.** Main path = emotional spine; optional path = epistemological spine.

## 7. How Communities Form Theories

The research on ARGs and collective solving (Fez, Tunic, Destiny's Corridors of Time, I Love Bees, Elden Ring's participatory lore culture) shows theory-communities need **preconditions**, not luck:

1. **Authored ambiguity.** Mystery must feel *ruled* — consistent dates, consistent iconography, numerically stable details. Fans build spreadsheets only when the data deserves spreadsheets. (Elden Ring scholarship documents "lore crafting" as a formal fan practice — Reddit as a distributed research lab.)
2. **Scale-locked puzzles.** Corridors of Time (2020) worked because *no individual could solve it*: thousands of players submitted screenshots into community spreadsheets; anyone could contribute regardless of skill. Bungie's own postmortem celebrated the distributed effort. Puzzles sized for communities create communities.
3. **Unreliable narrators + two supported readings.** Destiny's Unveiling, FromSoft's contradictory item texts, Silent Hill's psychology: when two mutually exclusive interpretations each have real evidence, the argument *is* the endgame.
4. **A visible clock or drift.** Fortnite's map changes, Destiny's weekly evolutions, live events that permanently alter the world: shared real-time change converts players into witnesses and witnesses into theorists.
5. **Developer silence as policy.** FromSoftware never adjudicates lore disputes. The moment devs confirm theories in interviews, the market for theorizing collapses.
6. **Findable fingerprints.** Communities canonize *recurring specifics*: a nine-note motif, a recurring date, a symbol in unrelated places. Give dataminers deliberate, authored discoveries (and never let datamining be the *only* path — Tunic's manual pages and in-game cipher show the honest version).

### Extracted Laws
- **L25. Design mysteries at community scale** — at least one puzzle no single player could reasonably solve.
- **L26. Maintain two defensible cosmologies in the evidence.** Feed both sides deliberately, for years.
- **L27. Choose a fingerprint** (a number, a rhythm, a symbol) and hide it with total consistency across every content channel from day one.
- **L28. Institutional silence.** The studio never confirms deep lore. In-fiction documents may lie; developers never speak.
- **L29. Let the world change on a public clock** — visible, slow, measurable drift that fans can chart.

## 8. How Expansions and Sequels Keep Stories Alive for Years

**FFXIV's model — the seed vault + NPC promotion.** Plant payoffs expansions early (L8's long-seed doctrine), and *promote supporting cast*: Estinien and G'raha Tia grew from side characters into protagonists across a decade, so the world feels like a repertory company rather than a villain-of-the-week machine.

**Destiny's model — escalating cosmology with a raid keystone.** Each year: one expansion (moves the cosmology), seasons (character maintenance + drip mystery), one raid (deepest lore + community event). The decade's lesson set is unusually complete:
- *What worked:* The Taken King, Forsaken, The Witch Queen — expansions built on a **personal villain + one cosmological reveal each**. The Final Shape's celebrated finale kept "a tight focus on the main cast" after years of learning that humanity beats sci-fi jargon.
- *What failed:* Curse of Osiris and Lightfall (filler arcs that spent mystery without minting any); **content vaulting** (deleting old campaigns destroyed the archive theorists depend on — evidence must stay in the record); and total closure (post-Final Shape retention collapse — see L10).

**WoW's model — the villain pipeline.** Each expansion *shows* the next threat early: Wrath of the Lich King let Arthas personally interrupt ordinary leveling zones for dozens of hours before players ever raided him. Familiarity, not surprise, made Icecrown mythic.

**The Kojima model — chronology as playground.** Metal Gear's timeline jumps decades either side of "now"; prequels can carry the saga's biggest revelations (MGS3, the best-regarded entry, is set 40 years before the series' present). A settled *past* with unsettled *meaning* is expansion fuel forever.

**The Zelda model — mythic looseness.** A deliberately loose timeline where every game is a retelling of a recurring pattern. Ambiguity at franchise scale as a *feature*: fans have argued the timeline for 25 years, and the arguing is the engagement.

### Extracted Laws
- **L30. Escalate mystery, not stakes.** Stakes inflate to absurdity in two expansions (galaxy → multiverse → ...). Depth escalation (what we thought was X is actually the skin of Y) is inexhaustible.
- **L31. One personal villain + one cosmological reveal per expansion.** Never more; never neither.
- **L32. Promote the supporting cast.** This year's quartermaster is Year 3's tragic protagonist. Plan the promotions.
- **L33. Never delete the archive.** Old campaigns, logs, and seasonal stories must stay playable/readable — theorists are your historians.
- **L34. Show the next era's threat inside this era's ordinary content**, at street level, long before it headlines.

## 9. How Campaigns Balance Emotion, Spectacle, Mystery, and Gameplay

**The intensity wave.** Naughty Dog, id, and Halo pacing analyses agree: sustained intensity numbs. Halo's classic "30 seconds of fun" combat loop was always alternated with quiet traversal and awe beats. The reference curve for a mission: *quiet (texture, character) → dread (mystery pressure) → burst (spectacle/combat) → aftermath (hold the silence)*. The aftermath phase is the most commonly cut and the most valuable — RDR2 and God of War (2018) both spend minutes of "walking and talking" *after* action peaks, and those minutes are where the story metabolizes.

**Spectacle must be anchored to a character-cost.** Shadow of the Colossus is the proof: sixteen boss spectacles, each staged as a *loss* (the music mourns your victory). Titanfall 2's "Effect and Cause" is the counter-proof from the other side: its time-swap spectacle is simultaneously a *mechanic*, so wonder and agency peak together. Spectacle that costs nothing and plays itself is a trailer, not a level.

**Hubs metabolize story.** The Normandy, Destiny's Tower, RDR2's camp, FFXIV's Rising Stones: a persistent social space where the cast reacts to events on a delay. Emotion in these games is processed *between* missions — cut the hub reactivity and the campaign feels like a playlist.

**Mystery pressure needs a gameplay valve.** Outer Wilds ties mystery directly to traversal; Subnautica ties it to depth progression (every biome deeper = darker truth + scarier fauna + better gear — dread, lore, and power on one shared axis); Control ties it to the file cabinet. When the mystery advances *only* in cutscenes, players stop feeling like investigators (see §0).

### Extracted Laws
- **L35. Author every mission on the four-beat wave** (quiet/dread/burst/aftermath) and protect the aftermath beat from being cut.
- **L36. Price every spectacle in character.** Someone pays for each set piece, on screen, in the fiction.
- **L37. Build a hub that reacts on a one-mission delay.** Grief, gossip, and doctrine changes appear in ambient dialogue after every story beat.
- **L38. Bind mystery to a gameplay axis** (depth, sync level, clearance tier) so investigation is played, not watched.

## 10. What Weaker Stories Got Wrong — the Anti-Pattern Catalog

1. **The unpaid mystery box.** Mysteries stacked without payoff schedule (documented in criticism of Expedition 33's mid-game and endemic to imitators of *Lost*). Fans forgive slow answers, never absent ones. *(Counter-law: L7.)*
2. **The over-answer.** MGSV's nanomachines, Death Stranding's final hours, any codex that explains the monster's biology to death. Answering the sacred core converts dread into trivia. *(L10.)*
3. **Stakes inflation.** Galaxy-ending threats by Year 2 leave nowhere to go (late WoW, Marvel-fatigued live services). *(L30.)*
4. **The emotional-contract retcon.** Halo 5 marketed a Chief/Cortana confrontation it didn't deliver and turned a beloved character villainous off-screen — the community never re-invested. Breaking an owned bond without on-screen earning is the costliest single mistake in franchise storytelling. *(L14.)*
5. **Amnesia as excuse.** Amnesiac protagonists deployed to skip worldbuilding (countless MMOs) rather than as an authored mystery with a checkable answer (KOTOR). 
6. **Gray sludge.** "Every faction is equally corrupt" reads as sophistication and plays as apathy — players need at least one thing worth loving (a squad, a city, a person) to make betrayal *cost*. 
7. **Ludonarrative rupture.** The charming rogue who machine-guns 800 men (Uncharted critique), the pacifist cutscene after a massacre level. Iron Breach's inverse risk: military grit in cutscenes, weightless shooting in play.
8. **Shock deaths without ledger entries.** Killing characters for marketing beats (or resurrecting them — Destiny partially refunding Cayde's death in Final Shape drew mixed response) — death must permanently change rosters, hubs, and systems or it's a stunt. *(L19.)*
9. **FOMO-vaulted narrative.** Destiny's Red War deletion is the canonical case: erased evidence, broken theory-chains, new-player onboarding vacuum. *(L33.)*
10. **Marketing the wrong promise.** No Man's Sky and Anthem shipped lore vacuums behind cinematic promises; Anthem specifically demonstrated that a live-service without a *narrative reason to return* retains nobody, regardless of gameplay feel.
11. **Dev-splaining.** Studios confirming/denying theories in interviews (or worse, in patch notes) — collapses the theory market the game spent years building. *(L28.)*
12. **Tone whiplash without an anchor cast.** Grimdark worlds need warmth somewhere (Souls has Solaire; RDR2 has the camp) or the audience's investment starves.
